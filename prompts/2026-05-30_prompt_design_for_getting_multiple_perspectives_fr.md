# Getting Multiple Perspectives from AI: Templates That Actually Work

When you need diverse viewpoints on a problem, AI can be surprisingly helpful—if you prompt it right. The key is being explicit about wanting different angles, not just asking the same question and hoping for variety.

## The Multi-Role Template

Instead of asking "What do you think about X?", try this:

```
I need perspectives on [specific problem/question] from these viewpoints:
- A [role 1] would focus on [specific concern]
- A [role 2] would prioritize [different concern]  
- A [role 3] would worry about [third concern]

Give me each perspective separately, highlighting where they conflict.
```

**Real example:** When evaluating a new team process, I asked for perspectives from "a busy manager focused on efficiency," "a new team member concerned about learning," and "a quality-focused senior developer." Got genuinely different takes that helped me see blind spots.

## The Devil's Advocate Approach

Follow up any initial response with:

```
Now argue against your previous recommendation. What are the strongest counterarguments? What could go wrong with this approach?
```

This works because AI tends to be helpful and agreeable by default. You have to explicitly ask it to challenge its own reasoning.

## The Stakeholder Spectrum

For business decisions, use:

```
Analyze [decision] from these stakeholder perspectives:
- Short-term financial impact
- Long-term strategic positioning  
- Employee experience
- Customer experience
- Technical feasibility

Where do these perspectives conflict? What are the hidden costs each group might not see?
```

## What Works and What Doesn't

**Works well:** Asking for specific professional lenses (lawyer vs. marketer vs. engineer). AI has decent models of how different roles think.

**Doesn't work:** Asking for "creative" or "out-of-the-box" thinking without structure. You'll get generic brainstorming fluff.

**Limitation to acknowledge:** AI perspectives aren't truly independent—they're variations from the same training data. Great for structured thinking, but don't mistake this for actual human diversity.

## The Follow-Up That Matters

Always end with: "Which perspective do you think gets overlooked most often in decisions like this?"

This question often surfaces the most valuable insight—the viewpoint that's systematically ignored in your context.

The goal isn't to replace human consultation, but to do better groundwork before those conversations happen.
