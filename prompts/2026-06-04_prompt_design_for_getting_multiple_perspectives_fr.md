# Getting Multiple Perspectives from AI: Three Patterns That Actually Work

When you need to explore different angles on a problem, AI can be surprisingly good at perspective-taking—but only if you structure the request thoughtfully. Here are three approaches I use regularly, with their real tradeoffs.

## The Role-Based Prompt

**Pattern:** Explicitly assign different roles or viewpoints in a single prompt.

```markdown
"Analyze this product launch strategy from three perspectives:
- As a skeptical investor focused on financial risks
- As an excited early adopter who loves new tech  
- As a cautious enterprise buyer with compliance concerns

For each perspective, highlight what they'd be most worried about."
```

**What works:** You get genuinely different concerns and priorities. The AI doesn't just repeat the same analysis three times—it actually shifts focus based on each role's motivations.

**What doesn't:** Sometimes the perspectives feel a bit theatrical rather than authentic. The "skeptical investor" might sound like a caricature rather than offering real financial insight.

## The Sequential Deep-Dive

**Pattern:** Ask for one perspective, then explicitly request the AI challenge or complement that view.

```markdown
First: "What are the strongest arguments for remote work?"
Then: "Now argue against those points from a manager who's seen remote work fail."
Finally: "What would a hybrid approach look like that addresses both sets of concerns?"
```

**What works:** Each response builds on the previous one, creating more nuanced analysis. You catch assumptions from the first response that might have gone unnoticed.

**What doesn't:** This takes more conversation turns, so it's slower. Also, the AI sometimes over-corrects in the opposite direction rather than finding genuine middle ground.

## The Stakeholder Mapping Approach

**Pattern:** Ask the AI to identify relevant stakeholders first, then provide each group's perspective.

```markdown
"I'm considering implementing AI tools at our company. First, identify all the stakeholders who would be affected. Then tell me what each group's primary concerns would be."
```

**What works:** This often reveals stakeholders you hadn't considered (like compliance teams, customer service, or IT security). The perspectives feel more comprehensive because you're not pre-selecting viewpoints.

**What doesn't:** Can get overwhelming with too many stakeholders. Also, the AI might identify obvious groups but miss subtle internal politics or cultural factors specific to your organization.

## Honest Limitations

AI perspectives are intellectually useful but lack the emotional weight and lived experience of real human viewpoints. A AI can tell you what a "concerned parent" might think, but it won't capture the visceral worry of someone whose actual child is affected.

Also, AI tends toward reasonable, moderate positions. If you need to understand extreme or passionate viewpoints—the kind that drive real human decisions—you might get sanitized versions rather than the raw intensity that matters.

**Bottom line:** These patterns work well for brainstorming, risk assessment, and checking blind spots. They're not substitutes for actual user research or stakeholder interviews, but they're excellent for rapid exploration when you're working solo.
