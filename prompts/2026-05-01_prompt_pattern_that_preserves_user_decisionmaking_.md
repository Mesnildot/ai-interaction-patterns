# Preserving User Autonomy in AI Interactions

## The Pattern: Options, Not Prescriptions

When working with AI daily, I've noticed a crucial difference between prompts that support decision-making versus those that hijack it. The key is structuring requests to expand your thinking rather than narrow it to a single "correct" answer.

**Instead of:** "What should I do about this customer complaint?"

**Try:** "Help me analyze this customer complaint from three angles: immediate resolution options, potential underlying issues, and long-term relationship impact. I'll decide the approach."

## Three Approaches That Preserve Control

### 1. The Analysis Framework
Structure prompts to get comprehensive breakdowns while keeping the decision yours:
- "Break down the pros and cons of [options A, B, C]"
- "What factors should I consider when deciding between X and Y?"
- "Help me think through the implications of this choice"

This works because you're asking for analytical support, not delegation of judgment.

### 2. The Multiple Perspectives Method
Ask the AI to show you different viewpoints instead of advocating for one:
- "How might a conservative investor, growth investor, and day trader each view this stock?"
- "Present three different philosophical approaches to handling this team conflict"
- "What would the marketing, engineering, and customer service teams each prioritize here?"

### 3. The Boundary-Setting Approach
Explicitly define what you want help with and what you're keeping:
- "Help me research pricing models for SaaS products. I'll decide what fits our market position."
- "Generate five email subject line options. I'll choose based on our brand voice."
- "Outline the technical requirements. I'll determine if they align with our resources."

## What Actually Works in Practice

The most effective autonomy-preserving prompts include:
- **Clear scope boundaries**: "Help me understand X, not decide Y"
- **Multiple options**: Always ask for 2-4 alternatives, never just one recommendation
- **Context separation**: Distinguish between information gathering and decision making
- **Explicit ownership**: Use phrases like "I'll evaluate" or "for my consideration"

## Common Pitfalls

**The advice trap**: Asking "What should I do?" consistently erodes your decision-making muscle. You start defaulting to AI judgment instead of building your own.

**False urgency**: Rushing to implement the first AI suggestion because it sounds authoritative. Good decisions need your context and values, which AI doesn't fully grasp.

**Over-reliance creep**: It's subtle but real. Start by asking AI to help you think, not think for you.

## The Honest Reality

This approach takes more time upfront. It's tempting to just ask "What's the answer?" and move on. But preserving your decision-making autonomy keeps you engaged with the actual problem-solving, maintains accountability for outcomes, and prevents the gradual erosion of critical thinking skills.

The goal isn't to avoid AI assistance—it's to use it as a thinking partner rather than a replacement brain.
