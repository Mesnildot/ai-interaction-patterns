# What Actually Works (and Doesn't) in Daily Human-AI Collaboration

After hundreds of hours collaborating with AI tools, I've learned that successful human-AI partnerships follow predictable patterns. Here's what I've observed working in the trenches.

## What Works Well

**Start conversations with context, not commands.** Instead of "Write a marketing email," try "I'm launching a productivity app for remote teams. The audience is skeptical of new tools. I need an email that acknowledges their tool fatigue while highlighting our unique approach." This upfront context investment pays dividends throughout the conversation.

**Treat AI as a thinking partner, not a magic oracle.** The best results come from iterative dialogue. I'll share a half-formed idea, get AI input, then build on it together. "What I'm thinking is X, but I'm stuck on Y. What angles am I missing?" This collaborative approach consistently produces better outcomes than trying to craft the perfect initial prompt.

**Be specific about format and constraints.** AI excels with clear parameters. "Write 3 bullet points, each under 15 words, focusing on benefits not features" works infinitely better than "summarize this." The more specific your requirements, the less back-and-forth you'll need.

## What Consistently Fails

**Asking AI to read your mind or fill massive gaps.** "Make this better" without explaining what "better" means wastes everyone's time. AI can't guess your unstated assumptions about tone, audience, or objectives.

**Expecting perfect first drafts.** I've learned to think of initial AI outputs as rough sketches, not finished products. Planning for 2-3 revision rounds sets realistic expectations and reduces frustration.

**Using AI for tasks requiring real-world verification.** I once had AI confidently provide completely wrong technical specifications. Now I double-check any claims about facts, current events, or domain-specific details through other sources.

## Three Practical Approaches That Work

**The "Show, Don't Tell" method:** Instead of describing what you want, share 2-3 examples of similar content you like, then ask AI to create something in that style.

**The "Constraint-First" approach:** Start by listing what you DON'T want. "No jargon, no buzzwords, nothing longer than 200 words." Negative constraints often guide AI more effectively than positive ones.

**The "Expert Interview" technique:** Ask AI to respond as a specific type of expert. "As a UX researcher with 10 years experience, what would you ask about this user feedback?" This frames the AI's knowledge more usefully than generic responses.

The biggest mindset shift? Stop trying to eliminate AI from the process and instead focus on where human judgment adds the most value - strategy, context, and final polish. AI handles the heavy lifting; humans handle the nuanced decisions.
