# Managing Attention and Cognitive Load with AI Tools

Working with AI tools all day can be surprisingly exhausting. I've found myself mentally drained after sessions that should have been productive, and it took some trial and error to understand why.

## The Hidden Cognitive Tax

AI interactions demand a specific type of mental energy. You're constantly context-switching between your domain expertise and prompt engineering, evaluating outputs for accuracy, and maintaining awareness of what the AI can and can't do. It's like having a very capable intern who needs detailed instructions and constant quality checks.

After particularly intense AI sessions, I notice the same fatigue I get from back-to-back meetings or complex problem-solving. The difference is it creeps up on you—the conversation feels effortless until suddenly you're burned out.

## What Actually Works

**Batch similar AI tasks together.** Instead of scattered prompts throughout the day, I block out 2-3 focused sessions. This reduces the mental overhead of constantly switching between "human thinking" and "AI collaboration" modes. One 90-minute session usually beats six 15-minute interactions.

**Set explicit stopping points.** AI conversations can spiral endlessly. Before starting, I decide: "I need three viable options" or "I'm spending 30 minutes on this." Without boundaries, perfectionism kicks in and you'll iterate forever.

**Use AI for drafts, not final outputs.** The sweet spot is getting AI to 70-80% of what you need, then finishing with human judgment. Trying to prompt your way to perfection is cognitively expensive and usually fails anyway.

## Managing the Collaboration Dynamic

**Alternate between generation and evaluation phases.** Don't try to critique while creating. Let the AI generate several options first, then switch to analysis mode. Mixing these mental modes is exhausting.

**Keep a "parking lot" for tangent ideas.** AI conversations often spark interesting side paths. Instead of following them immediately, jot them down. This prevents cognitive overload while preserving good ideas.

**Accept "good enough" more often.** AI makes iteration so easy that you can fall into endless refinement cycles. Sometimes the third draft is perfect; sometimes the first draft with minor human edits works fine.

## What Doesn't Work

Don't try to multitask during AI sessions—you'll miss subtle errors and waste time on poor outputs. Don't use AI when you're already mentally fatigued; the quality control requirements make it counterproductive. And don't expect AI to reduce your workload initially—it often redistributes effort rather than eliminating it.

The key insight: AI collaboration requires active mental energy, not passive consumption. Treat it like any other cognitively demanding work and plan accordingly.
