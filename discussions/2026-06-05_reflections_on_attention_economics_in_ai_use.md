# Attention Economics in Daily AI Use

After months of working with AI tools daily, I've noticed something unsettling: they can be incredibly effective while quietly fragmenting my attention in ways I didn't expect.

## The Hidden Cost of "Effortless" Help

AI tools promise to save time, and they do. But there's a cognitive tax that's easy to miss. When I ping ChatGPT for a quick explanation or ask Claude to review a draft, I'm not just trading time for convenience—I'm trading focused attention for distributed thinking.

The most insidious pattern: **micro-interruptions disguised as efficiency**. Instead of struggling through a problem for 10 minutes, I'll ask an AI after 30 seconds. Feels productive. Actually breaks my deep work rhythm and prevents me from building the mental model I'd get from wrestling with the problem myself.

## Three Approaches That Actually Work

**The Batch Method**: I collect AI-worthy tasks and process them in dedicated blocks. Write three rough drafts, then review them all with Claude. Research five related topics, then synthesize with AI help. This preserves sustained attention while still leveraging AI's strengths.

**The Timer Boundary**: Before opening an AI tool, I set a 5-minute timer and write out exactly what I need. This prevents the common spiral of "quick question" → extended chat session → completely derailed from original task. Works about 70% of the time.

**The Learning Tax**: For every AI shortcut, I force myself to understand one layer deeper. If GPT explains a concept, I find the original source. If it writes code, I trace through the logic. This maintains the learning muscle that AI can atrophy.

## What Doesn't Work

**Trying to use AI "mindfully"** while multitasking. The tools are designed to feel conversational and immediate. Fighting that design while context-switching just creates frustration.

**Blanket rules** like "no AI before noon." Context matters too much. Sometimes the AI interaction is the most focused thing I can do in that moment.

## The Real Tradeoff

The attention economy question isn't whether AI tools are net positive—they clearly are for most knowledge work. It's whether we're making intentional choices about when to think with them versus thinking through problems ourselves.

I've found that AI works best when I'm already in a generative state, not when I'm trying to bootstrap myself into one. The tools amplify good thinking but can't replace the messy, nonlinear process of developing ideas from scratch.

The key insight: **attention is finite, but it's also trainable**. Working with AI effectively requires developing new attention skills, not just new prompting skills.
