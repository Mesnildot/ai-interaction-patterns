# Maintaining Productive Tensions in AI Work

Working with AI daily has taught me that the most effective collaboration happens when you hold multiple contradictory truths simultaneously. It's not about finding balance—it's about maintaining productive tensions that keep you sharp.

## Trust, But Always Verify

I've learned to trust AI for rapid iteration while being ruthlessly skeptical of its outputs. This sounds contradictory, but it works. I'll use ChatGPT to brainstorm twenty different approaches to a problem, then systematically tear apart each suggestion. The AI gives me breadth; my critical evaluation provides depth.

What doesn't work is blind acceptance or complete dismissal. I've seen people waste hours fact-checking obviously correct formatting suggestions, and others ship AI-generated code without understanding what it does. Both extremes kill productivity.

## Speed vs. Understanding

AI excels at acceleration, but understanding can't be rushed. I maintain this tension by using AI to prototype rapidly, then deliberately slowing down to comprehend the core concepts. For example, when learning a new framework, I'll have AI generate working examples quickly, then spend time manually recreating key parts to ensure I understand the underlying patterns.

The trap is letting AI's speed become your only speed. I block out "slow work" time specifically for deep thinking without AI assistance. Otherwise, I risk becoming dependent on AI's pace and losing the ability to think deeply on my own.

## Automation with Human Judgment

I automate ruthlessly but keep human judgment in the loop at critical decision points. My AI handles routine tasks—formatting, initial research, boilerplate code—but I maintain checkpoints where human insight is essential.

This means designing workflows with explicit "human decision points" rather than trying to automate everything end-to-end. I've found that 80% automation with 20% strategic human input often outperforms attempts at 100% automation.

## The Expertise Paradox

Here's the tension I'm still figuring out: AI makes me more capable in areas where I have some knowledge, but can lead me astray in domains where I'm truly ignorant. The sweet spot seems to be using AI to extend expertise I already possess rather than to fake expertise I lack.

I use AI confidently for writing, editing, and programming because I can spot when it goes wrong. But I'm cautious about using it for legal advice or medical information where I can't evaluate the quality of responses.

## Managing These Tensions Daily

I've built specific practices to maintain these tensions:
- Time-boxing AI interactions to prevent over-reliance
- Keeping "human-only" thinking sessions
- Documenting what I learn without AI assistance
- Regular reflection on which tensions feel productive vs. exhausting

The goal isn't to resolve these tensions but to surf them productively.
