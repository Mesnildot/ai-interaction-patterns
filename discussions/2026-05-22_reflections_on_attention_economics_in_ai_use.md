# Managing Your Attention in the AI Age

Working with AI daily feels like being handed a superpower that comes with its own kryptonite: the constant pull on your attention. After months of integrating various AI tools into my workflow, I've learned that the biggest challenge isn't the technology—it's managing where my mental energy goes.

## The Attention Tax is Real

Every AI interaction has an upfront cost. You need to context-switch, frame your request clearly, and then evaluate the output. I've noticed this "attention tax" compounds quickly. Start the day asking Claude to help draft an email, then ChatGPT for research, then Copilot for code—suddenly you've spent an hour just managing AI conversations instead of doing deep work.

The worst part? AI makes shallow work feel productive. I can generate outlines, rewrite paragraphs, and brainstorm ideas rapid-fire. It *feels* like progress, but I'm often just churning through surface-level tasks while avoiding the hard thinking that actually moves projects forward.

## What Actually Works

**Batch AI interactions.** Instead of sprinkling AI requests throughout the day, I dedicate specific blocks to AI work. Usually 30-45 minutes in the morning for research and planning, then another chunk for revision and cleanup. This reduces context-switching overhead significantly.

**Be ruthless about scope.** AI excels at bounded problems but will gladly help you overthink unbounded ones. I've learned to catch myself when I start asking "What else should I consider about X?" These rabbit holes are seductive but rarely valuable.

**Use AI as a thinking partner, not a replacement.** The most valuable interactions happen when I come with half-formed ideas and use AI to stress-test or develop them further. When I abdicate the initial thinking entirely, the results are generic and require more cleanup.

## The Difficult Tradeoffs

The biggest tension I face is between AI's convenience and deep focus. AI can handle the tedious parts of writing, coding, and analysis, but it also fragments my attention span. I notice I'm less willing to sit with difficult problems when I know I can just ask AI for help.

There's also an opportunity cost issue. Time spent optimizing prompts or explaining context to AI is time not spent developing domain expertise. Sometimes the "inefficient" human approach builds knowledge that pays dividends later.

The key insight: AI doesn't just change what you can do—it changes how you think about problems. Being intentional about when and how you engage with AI tools isn't just about productivity; it's about preserving the kind of sustained, deep thinking that AI can assist but never replace.
