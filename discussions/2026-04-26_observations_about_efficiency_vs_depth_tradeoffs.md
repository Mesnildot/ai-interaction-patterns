# Efficiency vs Depth: The Daily Dance with AI Tools

After months of working with AI on everything from code reviews to content creation, I've noticed a consistent tension: the faster I want to go, the more I sacrifice depth and nuance. It's not that AI can't do deep work—it's that the incentive structure pushes toward quick iterations.

## The Speed Trap

When I'm debugging code, I can paste an error message and get a solution in seconds. But I've caught myself becoming lazy about understanding the underlying issue. The AI gives me the fix, I implement it, and I move on. Three weeks later, I hit the same pattern again because I never internalized what was actually happening.

The same thing happens with writing. I can generate a solid first draft quickly, but if I don't push back and ask follow-up questions, the output stays at that "good enough" level. The AI doesn't naturally go deeper unless I specifically guide it there.

## What Actually Works for Depth

**Multi-round conversations are essential.** Instead of expecting perfect output on the first try, I've learned to treat it like a conversation with a knowledgeable colleague. "Explain the reasoning behind that approach" or "What are three alternative ways to handle this?" consistently yields better results than trying to craft the perfect initial prompt.

**Context switching helps.** When I need depth, I explicitly tell the AI we're switching modes: "Let's slow down and really dig into this concept. I want to understand the fundamentals, not just get a quick answer." This simple framing changes the entire interaction quality.

**The 70% rule.** I've found that AI gets me to about 70% of where I need to be very quickly. The question is whether I need that extra 30%. For exploratory work or first drafts, 70% is often perfect. For critical decisions or learning something new, I need to budget time for that deeper dive.

## The Hidden Costs of Speed

The efficiency gains are real—I can prototype ideas, generate test cases, or explore topics much faster than before. But I've noticed my tolerance for the slow, uncertain process of deep thinking has decreased. When I sit down to work through a complex problem manually, the friction feels higher than it used to.

There's also a knowledge accumulation issue. When AI handles the intermediate steps, I miss the patterns and connections that come from doing the work myself. I'm more productive day-to-day but potentially less capable of handling novel problems without assistance.

## Finding Balance

I've started being more intentional about when to optimize for speed versus depth. Quick iterations for exploration and validation, slower conversations for learning and critical decisions. The tool is powerful enough that the choice is actually mine to make—if I'm deliberate about it.
