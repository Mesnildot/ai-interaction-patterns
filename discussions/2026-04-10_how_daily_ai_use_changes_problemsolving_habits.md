# How Daily AI Use Changes Problem-Solving Habits

After months of working with AI tools daily, I've noticed my problem-solving approach has fundamentally shifted—and not always in predictable ways.

## The "Pre-think" Habit

The biggest change is developing what I call "pre-thinking"—mentally structuring problems before engaging with AI. Instead of diving straight into brainstorming, I now pause to consider:
- What context will the AI need to understand this properly?
- Am I asking for exploration, validation, or execution help?
- What examples can I provide to guide the output?

This preparation step has improved both my AI interactions and my solo work. I'm more intentional about framing problems clearly, which leads to better solutions regardless of the tools involved.

## Iterative Thinking Becomes Default

Working with AI has made me much more comfortable with iterative problem-solving. Previously, I'd often try to think through entire solutions mentally before taking action. Now I start with rough approaches and refine through conversation.

**What works:** Starting with "here's my rough thinking" and building from there creates better momentum than trying to perfect initial ideas.

**What doesn't:** Over-relying on this can lead to shallow first attempts. Sometimes you still need that deep solo thinking before engaging any collaborator.

## The Verification Instinct

Daily AI use has heightened my verification habits across all work. I naturally double-check claims, cross-reference sources, and validate logic more systematically. This skeptical mindset carries over to human-generated content too.

The tradeoff: sometimes I over-verify obvious things, which can slow down momentum on straightforward tasks.

## Pattern Recognition vs. First Principles

AI excels at pattern matching, which has influenced how I approach familiar problems. I now instinctively look for similar situations and successful approaches before diving into first-principles analysis.

This works well for common business problems but can create blind spots for truly novel challenges. I've learned to explicitly ask myself: "Is this actually similar to X, or just superficially related?"

## Managing Cognitive Load Differently

Perhaps most surprisingly, regular AI collaboration has changed how I manage mental bandwidth. I'm more selective about what I hold in working memory, knowing I can offload certain types of thinking. This frees up mental space for higher-level strategy and creative connections.

The risk is becoming mentally lazy about details that matter. I've had to consciously maintain skills in areas where deep personal knowledge creates better judgment than AI-assisted analysis.

## The Bottom Line

Daily AI use has made me a more systematic, iterative problem-solver with better verification habits. But it requires conscious effort to maintain deep thinking skills and avoid over-dependence on pattern matching for novel challenges.
