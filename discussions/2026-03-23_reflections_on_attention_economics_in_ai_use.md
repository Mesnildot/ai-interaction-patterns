# Attention Economics in AI Use: What Actually Matters

After six months of heavy ChatGPT, Claude, and Cursor usage, I've noticed my attention patterns changing in ways I didn't expect. The promise of AI "saving time" is real, but it comes with hidden costs that are worth understanding.

## The Attention Trap is Real

The biggest surprise: AI tools can be *incredibly* attention-hungry. I catch myself in 20-minute rabbit holes trying to get the perfect prompt when I could have just written the damn thing. The "just one more refinement" loop is as addictive as any social media feed.

What works: Setting explicit time boundaries. I use a 5-minute timer for initial prompts. If I'm not getting useful output by then, I either switch approaches entirely or just do it manually. This sounds obvious, but it took me embarrassing amounts of lost afternoons to learn.

## Context Switching Costs

AI conversations create a weird cognitive load. Unlike Google searches that give you information to process, AI dialogue requires you to maintain context, evaluate responses, and guide the conversation. It's more mentally demanding than it appears.

I've found two patterns that help:
- **Batch similar tasks**: Instead of switching between writing, coding, and research throughout the day, I group AI-assisted work by type
- **Single-session completion**: For complex tasks, I try to finish them in one AI conversation rather than picking up threads later

## The Diminishing Returns Curve

There's a sweet spot in most AI interactions—usually the first 3-4 exchanges. After that, you're often polishing details that don't matter or going down paths that won't pan out. Learning to recognize this inflection point has probably saved me more time than any prompt engineering trick.

## What Actually Saves Attention

Three approaches that genuinely reduce cognitive overhead:

**Template-driven interaction**: I maintain a small collection of proven prompt structures for common tasks. Not because they're magical, but because they prevent me from reinventing the wheel each time.

**Quick validation checks**: Before diving deep with AI, I spend 30 seconds asking: "What would success look like here?" Often, I realize the task doesn't need AI at all.

**Parallel processing**: For research-heavy work, I'll start an AI conversation while also doing manual research. Whichever path yields results first wins. This prevents the "AI tunnel vision" where you assume it's the only way forward.

## The Real Tradeoff

AI tools excel at reducing certain types of cognitive load (remembering syntax, structuring content, exploring alternatives) while increasing others (prompt crafting, output evaluation, conversation management). The net benefit depends entirely on matching the right tool usage to your actual bottlenecks.

The key insight: AI doesn't automatically save attention—it redistributes it. Use it consciously.
