# Managing Your Attention in the Age of AI Tools

After two years of daily AI collaboration, I've learned that the biggest bottleneck isn't the AI's capabilities—it's managing my own attention effectively.

## The Attention Fragmentation Problem

AI tools excel at rapid iterations, but this becomes a trap. I've caught myself burning through dozens of prompt variations in minutes, chasing marginal improvements. What feels productive is actually attention scatter. My brain jumps between half-formed ideas without giving any single direction proper development time.

**What works better:** Set deliberate boundaries. I now limit myself to 3-5 iterations before stepping away to think. This forces me to be more thoughtful about each prompt rather than relying on quantity.

## The Context-Switching Tax

The most expensive attention cost isn't within a single AI conversation—it's jumping between different AI tools throughout the day. Each switch requires rebuilding context, explaining background, and recalibrating expectations.

**Two approaches that help:**
- **Session batching**: Group similar tasks to minimize tool-switching. Write all my code documentation in one Claude session, handle all email drafts in another.
- **Context documents**: Maintain shared context files that I can quickly paste to orient any AI tool to my current project state.

## The Review Paralysis Trap

AI output feels so polished that I over-scrutinize everything. I'll spend 20 minutes fact-checking a simple explanation that would have taken 5 minutes to verify from first principles. The cognitive overhead of "is this right?" becomes larger than just doing the work directly.

**What I've learned:** Trust but verify strategically. For high-stakes content, verification is essential. For brainstorming or first drafts, I let more slide and catch issues in later review cycles.

## Attention Recovery Strategies

The hardest part isn't using AI effectively—it's knowing when to stop. AI interactions feel infinitely extendable because there's always another angle to explore.

**Time boundaries work better than perfectionism:** I set 25-minute focused sessions with specific outcomes. "Generate three email templates" not "improve my email communication." Defined endpoints prevent the endless refinement spiral.

**Honest limitation:** I still struggle with the dopamine hit of AI responsiveness. It's genuinely harder to sit with difficult problems when instant feedback is available. Building tolerance for uncertainty and slow thinking remains an ongoing challenge.

The attention economy around AI isn't just about productivity—it's about preserving the cognitive space for deep work that only humans can do.
