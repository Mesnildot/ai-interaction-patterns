# Reflections on Attention Economics in AI Use

Working with AI tools daily has taught me that attention is the real bottleneck—not the AI's capabilities, but my own cognitive bandwidth to guide it effectively.

## The Hidden Cost of Context Switching

I used to jump between AI conversations constantly: coding help in one tab, writing assistance in another, brainstorming in a third. Each switch required rebuilding context, explaining background, and recalibrating the AI's understanding. The overhead was massive.

Now I batch similar work into longer sessions. When writing, I'll spend 30-60 minutes in one conversation, iterating on multiple pieces rather than starting fresh each time. The AI maintains context about my style, project goals, and constraints, making each subsequent exchange more productive.

The tradeoff? Sometimes I force-fit tasks into ongoing conversations when starting fresh would be cleaner. But usually, the context continuity wins.

## Prompt Fatigue is Real

After hours of crafting detailed prompts, I notice my instructions getting sloppy. The AI's responses degrade accordingly. It's like being a tired manager—your team reflects your diminished clarity.

I've learned to recognize the signs: vague requests, accepting mediocre outputs, not pushing back when the AI misses the mark. When I hit this wall, I either take a break or switch to more straightforward, factual queries that require less creative direction.

Some colleagues batch their AI work into focused blocks—intense sessions followed by breaks. Others prefer shorter, intermittent interactions throughout the day. Both work, depending on your natural attention rhythms.

## The Temptation of Over-Optimization

Early on, I spent enormous time perfecting prompts, tweaking parameters, and chasing marginal improvements. The attention cost often exceeded the benefit. A 15-minute prompt optimization might save 2 minutes on execution—terrible math.

Now I aim for "good enough" prompts that get me 80% of the way there, then iterate in conversation. It's faster and often produces better results because the AI can ask clarifying questions.

## What Actually Works

**Start sessions with clear intent.** I write a one-sentence goal before opening the AI tool. This prevents meandering conversations that eat attention without producing value.

**Use conversation threading.** Keep related work in the same conversation thread, but don't be afraid to start fresh when context becomes unwieldy.

**Build attention breaks into AI-heavy workflows.** The constant decision-making and evaluation required for AI collaboration is mentally taxing in ways traditional work isn't.

The irony is that AI tools can amplify your cognitive capacity, but only if you manage your attention deliberately. Otherwise, you're just outsourcing tasks while burning mental energy on coordination overhead.
